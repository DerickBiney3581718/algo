To write this formally, we use the standard structure of a **Proof by Induction** on the number of vertices $n$.

---

## Theorem

**In any tree $T = (V, E)$, there exists a unique simple path between any pair of vertices $u, v \in V$.**

---

### **1. Base Case ($n = 1$)**

Consider a tree with a single vertex $v_1$. The only pair of vertices is $(v_1, v_1)$. By definition, the path from a vertex to itself is the vertex itself (a path of length 0). Since no edges exist, no other path can be formed. Thus, the path is unique.
_(Note: If you prefer $n=2$ as a base case, a tree with two vertices must have exactly one edge $\{u, v\}$ to be connected and acyclic. This edge forms the unique path between them.)_

### **2. Inductive Hypothesis**

Assume that for some integer $k \ge 1$, every tree with $k$ vertices has a unique simple path between any pair of vertices.

### **3. Inductive Step ($n = k + 1$)**

Let $T$ be a tree with $k + 1$ vertices.

**Step A: Identify a Leaf**
A well-known property of trees with $n > 1$ is that they contain at least two **leaves** (vertices with degree 1). Let $v$ be a leaf of $T$, and let $w$ be the unique neighbor of $v$.

**Step B: Reduce the Tree**
If we remove vertex $v$ and the edge $\{v, w\}$, we obtain a new graph $T'$. Since $T$ was connected and acyclic, removing a leaf $v$ leaves $T'$ connected and acyclic. Therefore, $T'$ is a tree with $k$ vertices.

**Step C: Apply the Hypothesis**
By our inductive hypothesis, every pair of vertices in $T'$ has a unique simple path between them. Now, let’s consider any two vertices $x, y$ in the original tree $T$:

- **Case 1: $x, y \in T'$ (Neither is the leaf $v$)**
  The unique path between them in $T'$ still exists in $T$. Could a _new_ path have been created by adding $v$? No, because any path involving $v$ must travel to $v$ and immediately back through $w$ (e.g., $... \to w \to v \to w \to ...$), which would not be a **simple** path (it repeats vertex $w$ and edge $\{v, w\}$).

- **Case 2: One vertex is the leaf $v$ (Let $x = v$ and $y \in T'$)**
  To travel from $v$ to $y$, the first step **must** be the edge $\{v, w\}$ because $w$ is the only neighbor of $v$. From $w$, there exists a unique simple path to $y$ (by our hypothesis for $T'$).
  Concatenating the edge $\{v, w\}$ with the unique path $w \to y$ creates a path $v \to y$. Since the first step is mandatory and the remaining path is unique, the total path from $v$ to $y$ is unique.

### **4. Conclusion**

Since the property holds for $n=1$ and the inductive step shows it holds for $n=k+1$ whenever it holds for $n=k$, by the **Principle of Mathematical Induction**, the theorem is proven for all $n \ge 1$.
